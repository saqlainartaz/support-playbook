# Billing & Payments

Use this when a customer emails about a payment, a charge, updating their card, or a refund.

---

## Situation 1 — "When is my next payment, and which card will it use?"

**What they usually say:** They want to know when their next payment is due, and whether it'll
come off the card on file or a different one.

**What to do:**
1. Check the renewal date and the card on file in the billing system.
2. Confirm both back to them.
3. Offer to send the secure card-update link if they want to switch cards.

**Reply you can send:**
> Hi [their first name],
> Thanks for checking in. Your payment will auto-renew on [date], and it'll be charged to the
> card on file ending in [last 4 digits].
> If you'd prefer to use a different card, just let me know and I'll send over a secure link to
> update it before then.
> Best,

**Watch out for:**
- Never take card numbers over email — always send the secure card-update link instead.

---

## Situation 2 — A payment failed (reaching out first)

Use this when you need to let someone know their recurring payment didn't go through.

**Reply you can send:**
> Hey!
> I hope this email finds you well! I'm writing to let you know that your most recent recurring
> payment for our services was unsuccessful.
> Please don't worry – these things happen! Could you let us know how you'd like to handle this?
> If you need to update your credit card info or use a different card than the one we have on
> file, it's super easy!
> You can update your credit card info with this link: [secure card-update link]
> This form only collects info and does not charge your card. Once you're done, let me know so I
> can apply the new info to your account.
> If you have already spoken to a team member about your payment, please let me know in your
> reply so I can update our systems to reflect this.
> I look forward to your response today.
> Best regards,

**Watch out for:**
- Reassure them the form **only collects info — it doesn't charge the card.** It calms nerves.
- If they say they've already sorted payment with someone, note the account so they don't get
  chased again.

---

## Situation 3 — "I'm going through a hard time — can I pay later?"

**What they usually say:** They can't pay on time (a family or money hardship) and offer to pay
later, often doubling up next month.

**What to do:**
1. Lead with empathy.
2. If their plan is reasonable, agree to it.
3. Pause anything from going out in the meantime, and note the account.
4. Offer the payment link for when they're ready.

**Reply you can send:**
> Hi [their first name],
> Thanks for the update, and sorry to hear about [the situation]. That plan works. Make this
> month's payment when you can, and double up on the next one after [date]. I'll note your
> account, so nothing further goes out in the meantime.
> Just let me know when you're ready to make the payment and I'll send you the new link.
> Best,

**Watch out for:**
- If they mention something about their shoot or script in the same email, pass that note to the
  right team and tell them you've done so.
- Actually pause the billing — don't just say you will.

---

## Situation 4 — "I want a refund" / "I'm disputing this charge"

**What they usually say:** They're unhappy about a charge, want it refunded, or threaten to
dispute it with their bank.

**What to do:**
1. Stay calm and factual. Don't promise a refund or make contract decisions on your own.
2. Pull up their account history — contract, payment schedule, any recorded calls — so the
   facts are clear.
3. **Escalate to the account owner / billing lead right away**, ideally before they contact
   their bank, and let the customer know you're getting it sorted directly for them.

**Watch out for:**
- This one isn't yours to resolve alone. The goal is to keep it calm and get the right person on
  it fast.
